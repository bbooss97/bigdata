Unbiased estimator for population variance: clearly explained!

  • Post date July 15, 2020
  • Post categories In Pedagogical Note
  • Tags Probability, Statistics, Unbiased Estimator, Variance
  • 2 Comments on Unbiased estimator for population variance: clearly explained!

Estimator: A statistic used to approximate a population parameter. Sometimes called a point estimator. Estimate: The observed value of the estimator. Unbiased estimator: An estimator whose expected value is equal to the parameter that it is trying to estimate.

Sheldon M. Ross (2010).

This post is based on two YouTube videos made by the wonderful YouTuber jbstatistics

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7mYDHbrLEQo

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D1hgiAla3KI&list=WL&index=11&t=0s

The most pedagogical videos I found on this subject.

Sometimes, students wonder why we have to divide by n-1 in the formula of the sample variance. In this pedagogical post, I show why dividing by n-1 provides an unbiased estimator of the population variance which is unknown when I study a peculiar sample. I start with n independent observations with mean µ and variance σ2.

X1,X2,…,XnX_1, X_2, \dots, X_nX1​,X2​,…,Xn​

I recall that two important properties for the expected value:

E(ΣXi)=ΣE(Xi)E(cXi)=cE(Xi)E(ΣX_i)=ΣE(X_i) \\ E(cX_i)=cE(X_i)E(ΣXi​)=ΣE(Xi​)E(cXi​)=cE(Xi​)

The variance is defined as follows:

V(X)=E(X2)−[E(X)]2V(X)=E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2V(X)=E(X2)−[E(X)]2

Thus, I rearrange the variance formula to obtain the following expression:

E(X2)=V(X)+[E(X)]2E(X2)=σ2+μ2\begin{aligned} E(X^2)&=V(X)+[E(X)]^2 \\ E(X^2)&=\sigma^2+\mu^2 \end{aligned}E(X2)E(X2)​=V(X)+[E(X)]2=σ2+μ2​

For the proof I also need the expectation of the square of the sample mean:

E(Xˉ2)=V(Xˉ)+[E(Xˉ)]2E(\bar X^2)=V(\bar X)+[E(\bar X)]^2E(Xˉ2)=V(Xˉ)+[E(Xˉ)]2

Before moving further, I can find the expression for the expected value of the mean and the variance of the mean:

E(Xˉ)=E(X1+X2+⋯+Xnn)E(\bar X) = E\Big(\frac{X_1+X_2+\dots+X_n}{n}\Big)E(Xˉ)=E(nX1​+X2​+⋯+Xn​​)

The expected value operator is linear:

E(cXi)=cE(Xi)\begin{aligned} E(cX_i)&=cE(X_i) \end{aligned}E(cXi​)​=cE(Xi​)​
E(Xˉ)=(1n)(μ+μ+⋯+μ)E(Xˉ)=(1n)n×μE(Xˉ)=μ\begin{aligned} E(\bar X) &= \Big(\frac{1}{n}\Big)(\mu+\mu+\dots+\mu)\\ E(\bar X) &= \Big(\frac{1}{n}\Big)n\times\mu\\ E(\bar X) &= \mu \end{aligned} E(Xˉ)E(Xˉ)E(Xˉ)​=(n1​)(μ+μ+⋯+μ)=(n1​)n×μ=μ​

I move to the variance of the mean:

V(Xˉ)=V(X1+X2+⋯+Xnn)V(\bar X) = V\Big(\frac{X_1+X_2+\dots+X_n}{n}\Big)V(Xˉ)=V(nX1​+X2​+⋯+Xn​​)

Since the variance is a quadratic operator, I have:

V(cXi)=c2V(Xi)\begin{aligned} V(cX_i)&=c^2V(X_i) \end{aligned}V(cXi​)​=c2V(Xi​)​
V(Xˉ)=(1n)2(σ2+σ2+⋯+σ2)V(Xˉ)=(1n)2n×σ2V(Xˉ)=σ2n\begin{aligned} V(\bar X) &= \Big(\frac{1}{n}\Big)^2(\sigma^2+\sigma^2+\dots+\sigma^2)\\ V(\bar X) &= \Big(\frac{1}{n}\Big)^2n\times\sigma^2\\ V(\bar X) &= \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \end{aligned}V(Xˉ)V(Xˉ)V(Xˉ)​=(n1​)2(σ2+σ2+⋯+σ2)=(n1​)2n×σ2=nσ2​​

Thus, I obtain:

E(Xˉ2)=V(Xˉ)+[E(Xˉ)]2E(Xˉ2)=σ2n+μ2\begin{aligned} E(\bar X^2)&=V(\bar X)+[E(\bar X)]^2\\ E(\bar X^2)&=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}+\mu^2 \end{aligned}E(Xˉ2)E(Xˉ2)​=V(Xˉ)+[E(Xˉ)]2=nσ2​+μ2​

I need to show that:

E(S2)=E[Σi=1n(Xi−Xˉ)2n−1]=σ2\begin{aligned} E(S^2)&=E\Big[\frac{\Sigma_{i=1}^n (X_i-\bar X)^2}{n-1}\Big]\\ &=\sigma^2 \end{aligned}E(S2)​=E[n−1Σi=1n​(Xi​−Xˉ)2​]=σ2​

I focus on the expectation of the numerator, in the sum I omit the superscript and the subscript for clarity of exposition:

E[Σ(Xi−Xˉ)2]E[Σ(Xi2−2XiXˉ+Xˉ2)]\begin{aligned} E[\Sigma (X_i&-\bar X)^2]\\ E[\Sigma (X_{i}^{2}&-2X_{i}\bar X+\bar X^2)]\\ \end{aligned}E[Σ(Xi​E[Σ(Xi2​​−Xˉ)2]−2Xi​Xˉ+Xˉ2)]​

Because,

(a−b)2=(a2−2ab+b2)(a-b)^2=(a^2-2ab+b^2)(a−b)2=(a2−2ab+b2)

I continue by rearranging terms in the middle sum:

E[Σ(Xi2−2XiXˉ+Xˉ2)]E[ΣXi2−Σ2XiXˉ+ΣXˉ2]E[ΣXi2−2XˉΣXi+nXˉ2]E[ΣXi2−2XˉnXˉ+nXˉ2]\begin{aligned} E[\Sigma (X_{i}^{2}&-2X_{i}\bar X+\bar X^2)]\\ E[\Sigma X_{i}^{2}&-\Sigma 2X_{i}\bar X+\Sigma\bar X^2]\\ E[\Sigma X_{i}^{2}&-2\bar X\Sigma X_{i}+n\bar X^2]\\ E[\Sigma X_{i}^{2}&-2\bar Xn\bar X+n\bar X^2]\\ \end{aligned}E[Σ(Xi2​E[ΣXi2​E[ΣXi2​E[ΣXi2​​−2Xi​Xˉ+Xˉ2)]−Σ2Xi​Xˉ+ΣXˉ2]−2XˉΣXi​+nXˉ2]−2XˉnXˉ+nXˉ2]​

Remember that the mean is the sum of the observations divided by the number of the observations:

Xˉ=ΣXinΣXi=n×Xˉ\begin{aligned} \bar X&=\frac{\Sigma X_{i}}{n}\\ \Sigma X_{i}&=n\times\bar X \end{aligned}XˉΣXi​​=nΣXi​​=n×Xˉ​

I continue and since the expectation of the sum is equal to the sum of the expectation, I have:

E[ΣXi2−2XˉnXˉ+nXˉ2]E[ΣXi2−2nXˉ2+nXˉ2]E[ΣXi2−nXˉ2]E(ΣXi2)−E(nXˉ2)E(ΣXi2)−nE(Xˉ2)\begin{aligned} E[\Sigma X_{i}^{2}&-2\bar Xn\bar X+n\bar X^2]\\ E[\Sigma X_{i}^{2}&-2n\bar X^2+n\bar X^2]\\ E[\Sigma X_{i}^{2}&-n\bar X^2]\\ E(\Sigma X_{i}^{2})&-E(n\bar X^2)\\ E(\Sigma X_{i}^{2})&-nE(\bar X^2)\\ \end{aligned}E[ΣXi2​E[ΣXi2​E[ΣXi2​E(ΣXi2​)E(ΣXi2​)​−2XˉnXˉ+nXˉ2]−2nXˉ2+nXˉ2]−nXˉ2]−E(nXˉ2)−nE(Xˉ2)​

I use the results obtained earlier:

E(X2)=σ2+μ2E(Xˉ2)=σ2n+μ2\begin{aligned} E(X^2)&=\sigma^2+\mu^2\\ E(\bar X^2)&=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}+\mu^2 \end{aligned}E(X2)E(Xˉ2)​=σ2+μ2=nσ2​+μ2​
E[Σ(Xi−Xˉ)2]=E(ΣXi2)−nE(Xˉ2)=Σ(σ2+μ2)−n(σ2n+μ2)=nσ2+nμ2−σ2−nμ2=nσ2−σ2=(n−1)σ2\begin{gathered} \small E[\Sigma (X_i-\bar X)^2]= \small E(\Sigma X_{i}^{2})-nE(\bar X^2)\\ = \small \Sigma (\sigma^2+\mu^2)-n\Big(\frac{\sigma^2}{n}+\mu^2\Big)\\ = \small n\sigma^2+n\mu^2-\sigma^2-n\mu^2\\ = \small n\sigma^2-\sigma^2\\ = \small (n-1)\sigma^2 \end{gathered} E[Σ(Xi​−Xˉ)2]=E(ΣXi2​)−nE(Xˉ2)=Σ(σ2+μ2)−n(nσ2​+μ2)=nσ2+nμ2−σ2−nμ2=nσ2−σ2=(n−1)σ2​

I wanted to show this:

E(S2)=E[Σi=1n(Xi−Xˉ)2n−1]=σ2\begin{aligned} E(S^2)&=E\Big[\frac{\Sigma_{i=1}^n (X_i-\bar X)^2}{n-1}\Big]\\ &=\sigma^2 \end{aligned}E(S2)​=E[n−1Σi=1n​(Xi​−Xˉ)2​]=σ2​

I use the previous result to show that dividing by n-1 provides an unbiased estimator:

E(S2)=E[Σi=1n(Xi−Xˉ)2n−1]E(S2)=1n−1E[Σi=1n(Xi−Xˉ)2]E(S2)=1n−1(n−1)σ2E(S2)=σ2\begin{aligned} E(S^2)&=E\Big[\frac{\Sigma_{i=1}^n (X_i-\bar X)^2}{n-1}\Big]\\ E(S^2)&=\frac{1}{n-1}E[\Sigma_{i=1}^n (X_i-\bar X)^2]\\ E(S^2)&=\frac{1}{n-1}(n-1)\sigma^2\\ E(S^2)&=\sigma^2 \end{aligned}E(S2)E(S2)E(S2)E(S2)​=E[n−1Σi=1n​(Xi​−Xˉ)2​]=n−11​E[Σi=1n​(Xi​−Xˉ)2]=n−11​(n−1)σ2=σ2​

The expected value of the sample variance is equal to the population variance that is the definition of an unbiased estimator.
